There are various method for you to know!

Active Recall

Actively trying to remember information without looking at your notes or the textbook.
This strengthens memory and enhances long-term retention.

It forces your brain to retrieve information, strengthening the neural pathways related to that knowledge.

After reading a section, close your book and try to recall the main ideas. Flashcards are a popular tool for this method.

Feynman Technique

Explaining a concept in simple terms as if you were teaching it to someone else.

By simplifying complex ideas, you reinforce your own understanding and identify gaps in your knowledge.

Take a concept and try to explain it in plain language. If you struggle, revisit the material and refine your explanation.

Interleaved Practice

Mixing different topics or subjects in one study session rather than focusing on a single topic.

This method strengthens problem-solving skills and enhances the ability to apply knowledge in varied contexts.

Instead of focusing on one subject for hours, switch between subjects or types of problems (e.g., math, history, and science) during a study session.

Pomodoro Technique

Breaking study sessions into intervals (typically 25 minutes of work followed by a 5-minute break).

Short, focused bursts of study help maintain attention and prevent burnout.

Set a timer for 25 minutes, work on a task, then take a 5-minute break. After four "Pomodoros," take a longer break (15-30 minutes).

SQ3R Method
(Survey, Question, Read, Recite, Review)

A systematic approach to reading and retaining information from textbooks or other materials.

It breaks down the reading process into manageable steps, helping you retain more information.

How to apply:
Survey- Skim the material to get an overview.

Mind Mapping

Creating visual diagrams that link concepts together, helping you see relationships between ideas.

It helps you organize information visually, improving both comprehension and recall.

Use a blank sheet of paper or software tools to create a map with a central idea, branching out into related subtopics.
